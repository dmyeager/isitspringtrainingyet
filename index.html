THE MORNING HORSEHIDE HERALD

"Every Score Set Down, No Deed Unsung"

~ Being a Faithful Daily Chronicle of the National Pastime ~

Volume I · No. 12 · Monday, the Twentieth of July, Two Thousand and Twenty-Six

Reporting 16 contests from the day prior.

The Game of the Day

HONORS DIVIDED IN THE BRONX! YAMAMOTO GOES THE FULL ROUTE IN THE OPENER, 8 TO 2, BEFORE CHISHOLM'S EIGHTH-INNING THUNDER STEALS THE NIGHTCAP FOR THE PINSTRIPES, 2 TO 1

The twin bill the whole town awaited yields a masterpiece and a heart-stopper: the Japanese ace throws the first complete game of his six-and-sixty American starts and dents the league's stingiest arm, whereupon Jazz Chisholm Jr. answers Tommy Edman's tying blast with one of his own to split the day's accounts

The day-and-night settlement ordered in the Bronx delivered everything the connoisseurs were promised and a little they were not. In the opener, the duel of Yoshinobu Yamamoto against young Cam Schlittler — keeper of the American League's finest earned-run mark — held taut into the middle innings: Shohei Ohtani doubled home a run in the third and Freddie Freeman singled home another, Trent Grisham answered with his eleventh round-tripper, and Andy Pages restored the two-run cushion with a double in the fifth. Then the eighth arrived, and with it the deluge — a ground-rule double from Teoscar Hernández, a two-run single from Tommy Edman, singles and sacrifices besides, five runs in all against the New York relief corps — while Yamamoto simply declined to leave. Nine innings, four hits, two runs, not a walk among them, seven strikeouts: the first complete game of his six-and-sixty starts in this republic, and an 8-to-2 verdict that hung the loss on Schlittler despite his eight strikeouts.

The nightcap was cut from entirely different cloth — a bullpen quarrel of one-run margins. Cody Bellinger singled home Paul Goldschmidt in the very first, and that solitary tally stood like a lighthouse until the eighth, when Edman — a one-man nuisance all day — lined his second home run of the summer to knot the affair at one apiece. The knot held for precisely half an inning. Jazz Chisholm Jr. stepped in against the Los Angeles bullpen and deposited his fourteenth home run into the left-center seats, the decisive blow in a 2-to-1 decision sealed by David Bednar, with five New York pitchers conspiring on a four-hitter.

So the ledger reads even: a game apiece, the Trolley Dodgers still lords of the National West at three-and-sixty wins, the Pinstripes a game and a half behind Tampa Bay in the American East — and October, one suspects, still taking notes.

News Around the League

Ohtani's Knee Files a Grievance

A cloud upon the Los Angeles horizon: Shohei Ohtani is nursing soreness in his left knee and will not take the mound for his scheduled Wednesday turn, the club offering no date for the pitching half of the two-way wonder to resume his labors. The bat, at least, remains in rude health — he doubled and singled home runs in Sunday's opener even as the knee barked. The Herald prescribes caution; the pennant arithmetic prescribes hurry.

A Fortnight to the Deadline, and Every Wire Humming

Two weeks now separate the contenders from the trading deadline, and the rumor mills grind without rest — the sages counting a dozen names likely to change addresses before the month is out. The catcher Shea Langeliers of the Athletics figures prominently in the whispers, while the Reds and the Astros are said to be weighing their own inventories. In Atlanta, the general manager Alex Anthopoulos allows that his club's course will be steered by the regulars soon expected back from the infirmary — a buyer's language, if the Herald reads it rightly.

The Crown Watch: Alvarez Leads All Three Columns

Let the record show that Yordan Alvarez of Houston, whose club languishes below the level line, is himself conducting a one-man pennant race with history: his .319 average, his one-and-thirty home runs, and his three-and-seventy runs driven in each lead the American League outright. The Triple Crown has not been worn in that circuit since Miguel Cabrera in 2012; Alvarez added an RBI double to the cause on Sunday even in defeat. The Herald has taken to checking the leader boards each morning the way one checks the weather.

The Infirmary Ledger

Sorrier wires from the surgeons' wing. Baltimore, seven straight wins to the good, must now streak on without Ryan Helsley, removed to the sixty-day invalid list with discomfort in his throwing elbow; Detroit has likewise transferred the reliever Will Vest to the sixty-day rolls with inflammation in his elbow. In Kansas City the right-hander Stephen Kolek goes down with a flexor strain, and Houston has designated the oft-mended Nate Pearson for assignment — the churn of July sparing no roster its tax.

The Almanac: Six Days to Cooperstown

This coming Sunday, the six-and-twentieth of July, the National Baseball Hall of Fame conducts its induction ceremony at Cooperstown, ushering the newest immortals into the game's marble pantheon. And looking further down the calendar: the Field of Dreams game returns on the thirteenth of August, the Twins and the Phillies appointed to play amid the Iowa corn. The Herald's colors shall fly for both.

The Rest of the Card

THIRTEEN! THE FENWAY MEN HANDLE TAMPA BAY WITH EASE, 6 TO 1, FOR THEIR LONGEST STREAK SINCE NINETEEN-AND-FORTY-EIGHT

The streak has outgrown mere adjectives and entered the history books: Boston's 6-to-1 dispatch of the Gulf Coast club was a thirteenth consecutive triumph — the franchise's longest run since 1948, when the men of Ted Williams's vintage last strung so many. Sonny Gray labored through six innings of one-run ball to reach an improbable twelve victories against a single defeat, and Willson Contreras supplied the exclamation with a two-run clout, his two-and-twentieth. Tampa Bay's lone tally arrived by the scenic route — Victor Mesa Jr. legging out an inside-the-park home run in the second — but the Rays have now dropped five straight, their division lead shaved to a game and a half, while Boston perches above the wild-card line entirely.

NINETEEN RUNS! THE FRIARS VISIT AN APOCALYPSE UPON KANSAS CITY, 19 TO 2, WITH FRANCE'S GRAND SLAM ATOP THE RUBBLE

One day after being handled 6-to-1 by these same Missourians, the Friars of San Diego returned the insult with compound interest — nineteen runs upon nineteen hits, far and away the most lopsided verdict on the day's card. Ty France struck a grand slam in a five-run eighth and another homer in a six-run ninth for five runs driven in; Jackson Merrill went deep twice with four; Fernando Tatis Jr. knocked in four more with a homer among his blows. Jac Caglianone's early round-tripper briefly gave Kansas City pretensions. It did not last.

SEVEN RUNS DOWN AND UNDISMAYED: THE DESERT NINE STORM BACK UPON ST. LOUIS AND KEPLER ENDS IT IN THE TENTH, 8 TO 7

In the desert, a resurrection for the ages. The Redbirds of St. Louis led seven runs to none in the sixth — Blaze Jordan tripling and singling in the building of it — whereupon Arizona commenced the long climb: three runs on walks and singles in the sixth, a two-run inside-the-park home run from Geraldo Perdomo in the seventh, and Nolan Arenado's two-run, game-tying blast against his old employers in the eighth. In the tenth, Max Kepler lined the walk-off double that completed an 8-to-7 comeback from seven adrift — and the Snakes, winners of seven in their last ten, now breathe upon the wild-card line at half a game.

CONTRERAS EMPTIES THE BASES WITH ONE SWING OF THE NINTH; MILWAUKEE STEALS THE SWEEP, 3 TO 1

For eight innings the Cream City nine could do nothing whatever with Miami's pitching, trailing 1-to-0 on the strength of Esteury Ruiz's third-inning homer. Then came the home ninth, two men aboard, and William Contreras — who launched his tenth home run to center and his bat halfway to the foul line in the same motion, a three-run walk-off that completed the sweep of the series. Fairbanks wore the sudden loss; the Fish have now dropped six in succession, while Milwaukee, at two-and-sixty wins, owns the second-best ledger in all the land.

GOODMAN GOES DEEP THRICE AND LOSES ANYWAY; THE REDLEGS OUTLAST COLORADO, 9 TO 6

Rarely has a man done so much in a defeat. Hunter Goodman struck three home runs — his eight-and-twentieth through thirtieth — and drove in five, becoming the first Rockies player ever to author two three-homer games in a single season. Alas for him, Cincinnati had already banked eight runs inside two innings: a three-run blast from Nathaniel Lowe, the first of Tyler Stephenson's two homers, and Sal Stewart's three-run shot, his twentieth. Hunter Greene held the fort thereafter for a 9-to-6 verdict in the thin air.

SKENES BENDS CLEVELAND TO HIS WILL, AND THE ROOKIE VALDEZ WRITES HIMSELF INTO THE LEDGERS, 7 TO 1

Paul Skenes reminded the republic what the fuss is about — seven innings, one run, eight strikeouts — as the Buccaneers handled the Lake Erie men 7-to-1. Billy Cook had himself an afternoon, singling home runs in the second and sixth and even scoring on a pickoff gone wrong, before the rookie Esmerlyn Valdez applied the seal: a two-run, ninth-inning homer, his twelfth in but one-and-thirty games — making him just the fourth man in the annals to reach twelve homers and one-and-thirty runs driven in so soon upon arrival.

HENDERSON STEALS HOME! THE O'S TAKE A SEVENTH STRAIGHT, 5 TO 2, IN HOUSTON

The boldest larceny of the day belonged to Gunnar Henderson, who singled home a run in the third and then — with the crowd scarce believing it — stole home for good measure. Leody Taveras added a two-run single, Coby Mayo a solo blast, and Young pitched the Orioles to their seventh consecutive victory, 5 to 2, rising to eight wins against two defeats. Jose Altuve homered late for Houston, whose evening's one consolation was another RBI double from the crown-chasing Alvarez (see the news column).

BALDWIN PERFECT IN FIVE TRIPS, RILEY TWICE DEEP; ATLANTA OUTSLUGS THE LONE STAR NINE, 8 TO 5

The Tomahawk nine piled fourteen hits upon Texas, and two men did the heaviest lifting: Drake Baldwin went five-for-five with a pair of home runs — his seventeenth and eighteenth — while Austin Riley likewise homered twice and drove in four. Wyatt Langford answered with a homer and a double for the Lone Star men, but the arithmetic was never truly close after Atlanta's three-run seventh; Eovaldi absorbed the loss, and Texas clings to the American West's summit by but half a game over Seattle.

HOERNER CLEARS THE SACKS AND THE NORTH SIDERS BURY MINNESOTA, 10 TO 1

The North Siders scored early, often, and with a certain relish: Alex Bregman's two-run homer in the first — his torrid stretch continuing — opened the tap, and Nico Hoerner's bases-clearing triple in the third, his first three-bagger of the year en route to four runs driven in, blew the doors from the hinges. Ten runs in all, a single Minnesota tally in reply, and Shota Imanaga pocketed the win as Chicago, at six-and-fifty victories, keeps the senior circuit's first wild card in a comfortable grip.

BURKE AND THE PALE HOSE PAINT TORONTO IN WHITEWASH, 3 TO 0

The surprising Pale Hose, still atop the American Central, blanked the Maple Leaf nine 3-to-0 behind Burke's latest scoreless outing — the win his seventh against four defeats. Colson Montgomery provided the margin with his four-and-twentieth home run, Andrew Benintendi lofted home another, and Taylor barred the ninth for his fifth save while Toronto managed but four hits and no answer whatever.

McLEAN FANS TEN WITHOUT A RUN; THE METROPOLITANS ROUGH UP PHILADELPHIA, 6 TO 1

In Philadelphia the Metropolitans — buried sixteen games adrift, yet unbothered on this particular evening — piled up fourteen hits and rode Nolan McLean's gem to a 6-to-1 decision. The young right-hander fanned ten of the home nine without surrendering a run; Francisco Lindor homered in the first and drove in three; Juan Soto singled home another. Alec Bohm's solo shot was the whole of the home club's rejoinder, and the Phils let a chance slip to gain upon idle rivals in the crowded senior race.

ABRAMS AND CREWS GO BACK-TO-BACK-ADJACENT; THE CAPITAL NINE CLIMB TO THE LEVEL LINE, 5 TO 2

The Capital nine of Washington reached the .500 mark at fifty wins apiece with a 5-to-2 handling of the White Elephants, the fifth inning supplying the fireworks: CJ Abrams struck a two-run homer — a career-best one-and-twentieth — and Dylan Crews followed with a blast of his own. Young Harry Ford doubled home a run besides, and Griffin ran his mark to a gaudy eleven-and-two. The fleet Nasim Nuñez, twice a thief on the day, now counts seven-and-thirty stolen bases, most in either league.

THE HALOS HOLD THE DOOR BY A FINGER'S WIDTH, 3 TO 2, AS GREENE'S LATE BLAST FALLS ONE SHORT

Young Kevin McGonigle opened the affair with a first-inning homer, but the Halos chipped their way past — Zach Neto singling home the go-ahead run in the sixth — and then survived Riley Greene's ninth-inning round-tripper, his fourteenth, to hold a 3-to-2 verdict with Zeferjahn recording the final outs. A small victory in a long Anaheim summer; for the Bengals, a stumble after two days of good feeling.

GILBERT FANS TEN AND THE SOUND MEN OVERHAUL THE FRISCO NINE, 6 TO 3

San Francisco led early on the strength of Bryce Eldridge's first-inning homer — the towering rookie later adding the first stolen base of his career, evidently a completist — but Seattle reeled them in by degrees: Luke Raley and young Cole Young supplied home runs, Logan Gilbert struck out ten across six innings, and Muñoz fastened his seventeenth save upon a 6-to-3 decision. The mariners of the Sound now sit but half a game behind Texas in the American West.

Sixteen contests set down this morning, and what a morning's ledger it is: honors split in the great Bronx twin bill, Boston's streak stretched to thirteen — a figure unseen at Fenway since 1948 — nineteen Friar runs heaped upon Kansas City, and, by the Herald's count, two inside-the-park home runs legged out on a single afternoon, one apiece for Mesa and Perdomo. Tonight a full fifteen-game card resumes, and the desk marks three engagements in particular: the Dodgers open in Philadelphia against Cristopher Sánchez and his 2.62 mark, the streaking Fenway men welcome the seven-straight O's for a collision of hot hands, and the Rays limp into Toronto to face Cease. Until the next dispatch, keep your scorecards near and your enthusiasms nearer.
