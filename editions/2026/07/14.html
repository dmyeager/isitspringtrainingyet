Volume I · No. 6 · Tuesday, the Fourteenth of July, Two Thousand and Twenty-Six

No contests this day; the hot stove burns bright.

2 days until the Season's Second Half Commences (Thursday, the Sixteenth of July).

News Around the League

The Midsummer Classic, To Be Contested This Very Eve

The bats are stilled and the ledgers closed, for the season has reached its grand intermission — and upon this Tuesday eve the two circuits shall send their chosen paladins to do battle in the All-Star exhibition, that most gilded of summer pageants. The field of honor is Citizens Bank Park, hard by the Delaware, where the Brotherly Love nine keep their yard; the first pitch is appointed for eight o'clock in the evening, Eastern reckoning, and the whole of the baseball nation shall turn its lamplit gaze thither.

The pitching duel promises no mean spectacle. For the junior circuit toes the slab Dylan Cease of Toronto — six-and-four on the year against an earned-run mark of two-and-fifty-six-hundredths, and the game's foremost strikeout artist besides — while the senior league answers with a favorite son of the host town, Cristopher Sánchez of the Phillies, eleven-and-four with a glittering two-and-sixty-two. The American League sends the ageless Mike Trout to lead off from center and Houston's Yordan Alvarez — its reigning double-crown holder, one-and-thirty home runs and three-score-and-ten runs batted in — to follow in the cleanup berth. The National League counters with its own long-ball sovereign, Philadelphia's Kyle Schwarber and his two-and-thirty round-trippers, flanked by Juan Soto and the ever-graceful Freddie Freeman. No standing shall be altered, no pennant advanced nor lost; yet for one summer's night the finest arms and truest eyes in all the land are gathered under a single roof, and the Herald bids every faithful reader draw near his wireless.

The Pennant Picture, As the Clubs Pause to Draw Breath

The half-season ledger is set down, and it is the Boys in Blue of Los Angeles who bestride the whole of creation — one-and-sixty against six-and-thirty, their run-differential a positively imperial plus-one-hundred-forty-nine. In the senior circuit's Central the Brew Crew of Milwaukee reign at nine-and-fifty against seven-and-thirty, five lengths clear of the Cubbies, their own tally a stout plus-one-hundred-twenty-six; and in the East the Tomahawk nine of Atlanta hold sway by a pair of games over the host Quakers.

Across the junior loop the Sunshine nine of Tampa Bay top the East by three over the Pinstriped men of New York — whose plus-one-and-ninety is the finest run-mark in all the American League. The Central is a genuine deadlock, the White Stockings and the Lake Erie men of Cleveland knotted precisely at .526, neither able to gain a step. The West belongs, for the present, to the Lone Star nine of Texas by a game and a half over the mariners of the Sound. Mark, too, the Crimson Hose of Boston, who limp — nay, sprint — into the recess upon a nine-game winning skein and now scratch loudly at the Wild Card's door; and pity the Athletics, who stagger to the bench having dropped nine straight.

The Leaders' Ledger at the Intermission

In the American League, Houston's Yordan Alvarez wears a firm double crown, pacing the circuit in home runs at one-and-thirty and in runs batted in at three-score-and-ten; Tampa Bay's Yandy Díaz keeps the batting scepter at .322, Kansas City's Bobby Witt Jr. leads the base-thieves with a round thirty, Boston's Sonny Gray tops all winners at eleven, New York's Cam Schlittler owns the stingiest earned-run mark at two-and-five-hundredths, Toronto's Cease commands the strikeout column with eight-and-forty-and-a-hundred, and Cleveland's Cade Smith leads the firemen with eight-and-twenty saves.

In the senior circuit, Miami's Otto Lopez holds the loftiest average at a dazzling .334, Philadelphia's Kyle Schwarber wears the long-ball crown with two-and-thirty, St. Louis's Jordan Walker leads in runs batted in at four-and-seventy, Washington's Nuñez the stolen-base lead with four-and-thirty, and Milwaukee's Ashby the winner's lead at a round dozen. Grandest of all is that club's own Jacob Misiorowski, who at the break holds the twin crowns of earned-run average — a miserly one-and-sixty-two-hundredths — and strikeouts, seven-and-sixty-and-a-hundred, while San Diego's Mason Miller tops the National League's firemen with five-and-twenty saves.

Doings at the Wire

Even in the lull the telegraph key clattered on. Pittsburgh dispatched right-hander Cam Sanders to Baltimore in exchange for hard coin, whereupon the Birds promptly optioned him to their Norfolk affiliate. Baltimore also transferred left-hander Keegan Akin from the fifteen-day list to the sixty-day exile, his left elbow uncooperative, and the White Sox welcomed back right-hander Trevor Richards from bereavement leave.

A parade of the walking wounded set out upon the rehabilitation road as the recess afforded them room to mend — chief among them Atlanta's Ronald Acuña Jr., dispatched to the Florida Complex club alongside shortstop Ha-Seong Kim; Detroit sent second baseman Gleyber Torres and right-hander Jackson Jobe upon like errands, while Washington's Jake Irvin, Houston's Bennett Sousa, and Cincinnati's Dane Myers each took their first steps back toward the big show.

And so the National Pastime pauses to catch its breath, the scorebooks laid open at the half-year mark and no fresh tally to inscribe this morning — only the grand exhibition to come this very night beneath the Philadelphia lamps. We note the Dodgers astride all creation, the Brewers and Braves and Rays and the deadlocked Central pair holding their several thrones, and the Boston nine carrying a nine-game fire into the recess. Rest ye well, for the true hostilities resume anon. Until the morrow's dispatch, keep your scorecards near and your enthusiasms nearer.
