Volume I · No. 16 · Friday, the Four-and-Twentieth of July, Two Thousand and Twenty-Six

Reporting 5 contests from the day prior.

The Game of the Day

THE UNDERSTUDY STEALS THE SHOW: LOCKLEAR, PRESSED INTO THE LINEUP AT THE ELEVENTH HOUR, WRECKS ST. LOUIS WITH HIS FIRST HOME RUN OF THE YEAR, AND THE SNAKES ROLL ON, 10 TO 6

A sixteen-hit cannonade at Busch Stadium — the substitute's go-ahead blast in the fourth, a five-run eighth to salt the affair — and Arizona, winner of three running, tightens its grip upon the National League's last wild-card chair at the Redbirds' direct expense

Tyler Locklear had no business in the middle of this story. The young first baseman was pressed into Arizona's order at the eleventh hour, the wires report, when Ketel Marte was scratched with a stiffness of the back — and by evening's end the understudy had delivered the finest afternoon of anybody's week: three hits in five trips, three runs driven home, his first double of the campaign, and, in the fourth inning, his first home run of the year — a line-drive, two-run affair into the left-field corner that turned a 2-to-1 deficit into a lead Arizona never returned. St. Louis had struck first, two runs in the opening frame on Jordan Walker's run-scoring double and a Lars Nootbaar single — this moments after the Busch faithful rose to salute Walker's Home Run Derby heroics, his first home engagement since — but from the fourth onward the visitors ran the game like a counting-house.

The eighth inning was the ledger's cruelest page: five runs, struck in single blows like a drumroll — Adrian Del Castillo, Locklear again, the venerable Nolan Arenado off the bench, a Corbin Carroll sacrifice fly, Gabriel Moreno — and Arenado cashed another in the ninth for good measure, two hits and two runs batted in his brief evening's employment. Brandon Pfaadt carried the victory, six innings of two-run labor for his fifth win against a single defeat, and the 10-to-2 lead proved just deep enough to absorb a four-run St. Louis ninth, Lars Nootbaar driving home three on the night in the losing cause. Sixteen hits for Arizona in all; 10 to 6 the final.

Mark the standings, for this one carried freight: the Diamondbacks, winners of three straight and eight of their last ten, awoke at four-and-fifty wins against nine-and-forty defeats, alone in the National League's final wild-card chair — while the Redbirds they beat slipped a game and a half back of it, with Pittsburgh between them at a single game. The trading deadline stands ten days off, and Arizona is making its argument in the plainest language there is.

News Around the League

Ten Days to the Deadline: Names on Every Tongue, and the First Ink Is Dry

The bazaar of the third of August stands ten days distant, and the wires groan under the weight of names. The Halos, it is reported, have opened the bidding upon their slugging shortstop Zach Neto, and the Fenway men are said to be foremost among the appraisers. The sages spend their columns matching the Bay nine's batting artist Luis Arraez — he of the .320 average — to a half-dozen suitors, and the defining riddle of the season remains Tarik Skubal of Detroit: whether the Bengals will sell their ace at all, and what precisely a buyer would be purchasing from a man not three months removed from the surgeon's table — a question Skubal himself may address from the hill this very evening. One deal is already inked: the Buccaneers have acquired the reliever Ron Marinaccio from San Diego, showing the veteran Dennis Santana the door to make room. And the scribes note the root of all this fevered commerce: an improbable four-and-twenty clubs entered Thursday within six games and a half of a postseason berth, which makes sellers scarce and prices dear.

New Money for the White Elephants; A Young Slugger Chooses the Fish

Two items from the game's counting-houses. An investment company styled Harbinger Sports Partners — numbering among its principals Mark Cuban, late the majority owner of Dallas's basketball concern — has purchased a minority stake in the Athletics, as that wandering franchise steers toward its Las Vegas harbor in twenty-and-twenty-eight. And in Miami, the celebrated young Japanese slugger Rintaro Sasaki has signed with the Marlins, declaring that the Fish offered the quickest road to the big leagues — quicker than a return to his Stanford studies, and quicker than the professional circuits of his homeland.

The Champions Call at the White House

The Dodgers, twice-crowned champions of the world, paid their visit to the White House on Thursday — the second such call in as many years — where the President feted Shohei Ohtani, Freddie Freeman, and the assembled Angelenos in the East Room's best style. The club, the wires observe, harbors ambitions of earning a third invitation; the rest of the National League harbors opinions on the matter.

The Infirmary Ward: Toronto's Crowded Sickbay, and Convalescents on the March

The Maple Leaf nine won on the field (see the card below) but bled in the training room: the left-hander Patrick Corbin goes to the fifteen-day invalid list with a strain of the teres major, and the outfielder Addison Barger, his right elbow inflamed, is transferred to the sixty-day rolls. Elsewhere the traffic runs happily the other way — Colorado's center fielder Brenton Doyle has begun a rehabilitation engagement at Albuquerque, the Mets' right-hander Clay Holmes the same at Syracuse, and Miami has activated the left-hander John King from the invalid list. Arizona's Ketel Marte, scratched with back stiffness (see above), is listed among the day-to-day worriments rather than the wounded.

The Almanac: Cooperstown in Two Days, and a Cornfield in August

The household calendar, freshly consulted. Cooperstown's induction ceremonies stand but two days off, this Sunday the six-and-twentieth, when the game gathers to crown its immortals. On the thirteenth of August the Twins and Phillies will play the Field of Dreams game amid the corn at Dyersville, Iowa — the pastime's annual pilgrimage to its own mythology. And the far column of the almanac keeps its steady warning: the regular campaign closes on the seven-and-twentieth of September, two months and three days hence, and the wild-card arithmetic above suggests no shortage of clubs who will need every hour of it.

The Rest of the Card

SALE FANS ELEVEN, FOUR TOMAHAWK THUNDERCLAPS ANSWER, AND ATLANTA HOLDS OFF THE FRIARS, 6 TO 5

San Diego built a 3-to-0 lead in the second inning by patient means — a bases-loaded walk to Fernando Tatis Jr., a two-run single from Luis Rengifo — and then watched Atlanta answer entirely by air: home runs from Michael Harris II, Ozzie Albies, Dominic Smith (a three-run blow that turned the game), and Drake Baldwin, four in all. Chris Sale, ancient and unbothered, struck out eleven men across six innings for his eleventh victory, his earned-run average resting at 2.19, and the scribes have begun composing his Cy Young arguments in advance. Ty France's two-run homer in the eighth drew the Friars within one, but Raisel Iglesias banked the final three outs for his one-and-twentieth save. The Tomahawk nine, at sixty wins against two-and-forty defeats, lead the National East by four games and a half.

TWELVE INNINGS ON LAKE ERIE: WILLIAMS FANS ELEVEN FOR NAUGHT, AND THE MINNESOTANS TAKE THE LONG GAME, 3 TO 1

Cleveland's Gavin Williams threw seven innings of two-hit, scoreless ball and struck out eleven — and his club paid him in counterfeit, mustering three hits the whole of the night. Minnesota's Bradley matched him nearly stride for stride across seven frames, Patrick Bailey's fifth home run the lone blemish, and after Alan Roden squared the account in the eighth the two bullpens traded zeroes deep into the Ohio evening. In the twelfth, against the unfortunate Sabrowski, the visitors settled it — run-scoring singles from Trevor Larnach and Luke Keaschall, the second aided by a throwing error — and Anderson recorded the first save of his campaign. The Lake Erie men have now dropped two straight and sit a game and a half behind Chicago in the Central; the Minnesotans, winners of two running, head home with their spirits conspicuously repaired.

BIEBER IN THE OLD VINTAGE: SEVEN INNINGS OF ONE-RUN CRAFT, AND THE MAPLE LEAF NINE QUIET THE LEAGUE'S BEST, 3 TO 1

Shane Bieber, whose June return from the invalid list began haltingly, produced his finest turn of the season: seven innings, three hits, a single run, and not one base on balls, as Toronto subdued the American League's leading nine. The runs came a single at a time — Alejandro Kirk in the first, George Springer in the third, Vladimir Guerrero Jr. in the seventh — and Varland closed the account for his one-and-twentieth save. The verdict ends a four-game Toronto skid and trims the Gulf Coast club's cushion atop the American East to two games over the idle Yankees, who arrive in Philadelphia this evening with designs upon further trimming.

THE BENGALS RALLY TWICE: OUTMAN'S THREE-RUN BLAST AND DINGLER'S GO-AHEAD DOUBLE TURN BACK KANSAS CITY, 4 TO 3

For six innings the Missourians held the upper hand, Isaac Collins's two-run homer the difference and Randy Dobnak spinning five scoreless frames — and then Detroit answered in installments. James Outman struck a three-run homer in the seventh; Josh Rojas tied the affair back up in the eighth; and in the home half Dillon Dingler lined his two-and-twentieth double to cash the go-ahead run, his six-and-sixtieth run driven in, which ties him among the American League's leading providers. The same battery of Troy Melton and Dingler had earlier escaped a jam by the rarest of exits — a strikeout converted into a putout at the plate, the catcher throwing to the pitcher to retire a runner foolhardy enough to test him. Finnegan sealed the 4-to-3 verdict, and the surging Bengals have won two straight and six of ten, with the deadline sages watching their every move.

But five contests graced yesterday's card, and the Herald's compositors enjoyed the rest — for tonight the full fifteen-game bill returns. The eye falls first upon Philadelphia, where the Bronx Bombers open a series against the Phillies, Warren against Luzardo; upon Flushing, where the twice-crowned Dodgers call on the Mets; upon Detroit, where Tarik Skubal takes the hill with half the league's scouts in the grandstand; and upon Arlington, where the Mariners and Rangers meet with the American West's summit — a mere half-game's difference — hanging in the balance. The Guardians, twice-stung, visit the league-leading Rays besides. Until the morrow's dispatch, keep your scorecards near and your enthusiasms nearer.
