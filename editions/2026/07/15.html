Volume I · No. 7 · Wednesday, the Fifteenth of July, Two Thousand and Twenty-Six

Reporting 1 contest from the day prior.

The Game of the Day

THE JUNIOR CIRCUIT WHITEWASHES THE SENIOR, FOUR TO NAUGHT

In the Midsummer Exhibition Beneath the Citizens Bank Lamps, an Unbroken File of American League Arms Fan Fifteen and Anoint the Bronx Man Bellinger Their Prince

The great summer show, trumpeted these many weeks, proved no gentle pageant but a downright throttling. Before a congregation of three-and-forty thousand nine hundred and sixteen assembled hard by the Delaware, the American League's chosen fell upon their senior rivals in the very first frame and never once eased their grip, carrying the night four runs to naught. The host town's own favorite, Cristopher Sánchez of the Phils, toed the slab for the National League and found the footing treacherous at once: with a brace of runners aboard, the Angeleno-turned-Bronx man Cody Bellinger lashed a two-run single to center, home trotting Yordan Alvarez and Shea Langeliers; hard upon it Ben Rice singled home Bobby Witt Jr., and the reckoning stood three-to-naught ere the senior loop had fairly drawn breath.

From that opening blow the National bats were but a rumor — three hits all told, not a run to show, and five-and-ten times set down upon strikes by a relentless, unbroken file of eleven junior-loop arms, from Toronto's Dylan Cease through the last of the relief brigade. In the eighth the Pale Hose youngster Miguel Vargas, lately entered at the third station, launched a solitary shot to left for the fourth and final marker. Cease bore off the decision at one-and-naught with a clean frame and three men fanned, while Sánchez wore the loss. And when the smoke had lifted it was Bellinger — two runs driven home upon his lone base knock — whom the assembled scribes crowned Most Valuable, a Bronx man made prince of the summer's one true all-star night. No standing stirred nor pennant moved; yet bragging rights, that most imperishable of coin, rode home this morning in an American League valise.

News Around the League

The Wire Would Not Rest Through the Recess

Though the diamonds lay dark, the telegraph key clattered on. The Cream City nine of Milwaukee shipped right-hander Easton McGee to Kansas City for hard coin, while the Bengals of Detroit dealt outfielder Jahmai Jones to Boston for a player to be named in some later dispatch. The Royals, meanwhile, moved right-hander Nick Mears from the fifteen-day list to the sixty-day exile, a stubborn right-shoulder impingement the culprit. Small barter in the season's grand ledger, perhaps — but proof that even in the lull the clubhouses keep their commerce.

The Convalescents Take the Mending Road

The pause afforded the walking wounded room to heal, and a small caravan set out upon the rehabilitation trail. Minnesota dispatched right-handers Cole Sands and Garrett Acton to its Florida Complex nine; Washington sent Max Kranick and Trevor Williams upon like errands; and the Boys in Blue of Los Angeles started their own Edwin Díaz back along the Arizona Complex road. Elsewhere the roster-keepers tidied their books against the resumption — the Metropolitans outrighted Dan Hammer to Syracuse, the North Siders sent Jake Woodford down to Iowa, the Nationals passed Matt Krook to Rochester, and the Hub nine optioned young Nate Eaton to Worcester.

Sundry Doings About the League

From Philadelphia came word that the Rays' Junior Caminero, plunked by a pitch amid the exhibition, was pronounced sound — the plates showing naught amiss, to the relief of the Tampa faithful. The amateur draft's very first selection, young Cholowsky, put his name to a contract said to carry a record bounty for a top pick. And in the game's counting-houses the players' brotherhood raised its voice against an advertising campaign trumpeting a salary ceiling, calling the effort perverse — a reminder that not every contest this summer is settled between the white lines.

And so the summer's one manufactured quarrel is settled, the junior circuit's arms having blanked their senior betters four-to-naught beneath the Philadelphia lamps, with the Bronx man Bellinger bearing off the laurels. It counts for nothing in the pennant arithmetic and for everything in the taproom argument — which is, after all, the whole delicious point of the thing. Nor need the faithful wait long for the true reckoning: the second half opens this very Thursday, the Sixteenth of July, with the Metropolitans calling upon the Phillies by the Delaware, and the full card unfurling in its sixteen-fold glory come Friday. Sharpen your pencils. Until the morrow's dispatch, keep your scorecards near and your enthusiasms nearer.
